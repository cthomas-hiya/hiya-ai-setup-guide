Create a Project in ChatGPT

Required

✦ What is a Project — and why does it change everything?

Most people use AI like a vending machine: put in a request, get out a response, forget it happened. A Project breaks that pattern. It's a persistent workspace that remembers your role, your communication style, your key audiences, and your reference documents — across every conversation, forever. Instead of re-explaining yourself every time, you just start working. The AI already knows who you are.

Open ChatGPT and find Projects

Go to chatgpt.com and log in with your Hiya account. In the left sidebar, look for Projects and click "New Project." If you don't see it, make sure you're logged in with your Hiya work account — Hiya's Enterprise plan unlocks Projects for everyone.

Name your Project

Give it a name that reflects its purpose — Work Communications, Finance Comms, or GTM Messaging all work. Start with one that covers your most common needs; you can create more later.

Set the Memory option

Before clicking Create, click the gear icon inside the creation dialog to reveal the Memory setting. You'll see two options — choose Project-only. This keeps your project's context self-contained, so the AI's knowledge and memory for this project stays focused on your work rather than pulling in context from unrelated conversations. This setting cannot be changed after the project is created.

✦ What does Project-only mean?

Default allows the project to share memory with your other ChatGPT chats. Project-only keeps the project's memory isolated — what happens in this project stays in this project. For a focused work tool, Project-only is the right choice.

Create the project

Click Create. You'll land inside your new project. You'll add Instructions and sources in the next two sections — no need to do anything else here yet.

✦ Why ChatGPT for this path?

Hiya's Enterprise plan is already paid for and gives you everything Path A needs — Projects, persistent context, file uploads, data analysis, and privacy protections. If you find you prefer Claude's writing style after using both, Claude.ai Pro ($20/mo) supports the same Project workflow and is worth exploring as an optional upgrade.

Write your Instructions

Required

✦ This is the actual build

A system prompt is the standing brief you give the AI before every conversation — your role, your context, your communication style, your expectations. It transforms a generic chatbot into something that feels like a knowledgeable colleague who already knows your work. This is what most people never set up — and the reason they still feel like they're getting generic output.

To add Instructions, open your project, click the ⋯ (three-dot) menu in the top-right corner, and select Project settings. You'll see an Instructions field — this is where you paste the template below. Fill in the italicized parts as specifically as you can; the more detail you give, the better the AI performs.

📋 Instructions template — copy and customize

Your role and context

You are a professional communications assistant for [your name], who works as a [your role, e.g. Senior Finance Manager / GTM Lead / Revenue Operations Manager] at Hiya — a B2B SaaS company that sells call identification and branding solutions to businesses. My team is [your team]. My work primarily involves [2-3 sentences about what you actually do day-to-day — the more specific, the better].

Your communication style

When writing on my behalf, match this style: - Tone: [e.g. direct and concise / warm but professional / data-driven and precise] - Length: [e.g. short — never more than 3 paragraphs unless the situation warrants more] - Voice: [e.g. I avoid buzzwords / I lead with the ask / I use bullets for 3+ items]

Your key audiences

My main audiences are: - [e.g. Executive leadership — bottom line upfront, minimal context-setting] - [e.g. Cross-functional partners in Engineering and Product — prefer specifics] - [e.g. External vendors and customers — professional, relationship-aware]

Standing instructions

When I ask you to draft something: 1. Ask clarifying questions first if the request is ambiguous — don't assume 2. Offer a short and long version when the situation could go either way 3. Push back if my ask seems unclear or if there's a better approach 4. Never pad — if it can be said in fewer words, use fewer words When I share a document for review or summarization: 1. Lead with a 2-3 sentence TL;DR 2. Follow with key decisions, action items, and open questions 3. Flag anything that looks like it needs my attention or response

✦ Your Instructions are a living document

Your first version won't be perfect — that's expected. Every time the output misses the mark, go back to Project settings and update the Instructions field. After a few weeks of real use, you'll have something highly tuned. You can also ask the AI to help you improve it: paste your current instructions into the chat and say "What's missing from this that would help you write better on my behalf?"

Attach your Reference Docs

Optional but powerful

✦ Give the AI your source material

Files attached to a Project are available in every conversation within it — the AI can reference them without you re-uploading each time. A well-stocked Project is like giving a new team member a thorough onboarding packet: they stop asking basic questions and start contributing from day one.

Inside your project, click the Sources tab, then click Add. You can upload files directly, link a Google Drive folder, or connect one of Hiya's approved apps. Good candidates for sources:

  • All teamsYour team's standard communication templates or preferred formats — so output is always structured the way your team expects.
  • FinanceA glossary of internal terms, account names, or reporting definitions — so the AI interprets your numbers correctly.
  • GTMYour messaging guide, ICP description, or competitive positioning doc — so the AI writes with accurate product and market context.
  • All teamsA short "about the team" note: key stakeholders, how decisions get made, what's currently being prioritized. The context you'd give a new hire on day one.

✦ Hiya's approved app integrations

When you click Add in the Sources tab, look for the "For Hiya, Inc" tab to see apps Hiya has already approved for use as sources. These include Gmail, Google Drive, Google Calendar, Google Contacts, GitHub, Slack, HubSpot, Figma, Notion, ZoomInfo, and others. Connecting an app lets ChatGPT pull live context from it — for example, referencing recent emails or calendar events without you needing to paste them in manually.

⚠ Watch what you connect

Don't attach documents containing personal employee data, customer PII, or anything marked confidential beyond your access level. When in doubt, check with your manager first.

Path A · Your first system

Build a Communication Workflow ✦

Hands-on

✦ What you're building

A configured AI system that handles the full arc of a communication challenge — not just "rewrite this email," but "understand what I'm trying to achieve, reason through the best approach, and help me plan and execute." You'll use your Project as the foundation and build the workflow through a single conversation.

🎯 The exercise: A message you've been putting off

Think of a real communication task you've been avoiding — a status update that needs careful framing, a difficult ask, a message that lands differently for different audiences. Open a new conversation inside your Project and paste this prompt:

Your first workflow prompt
I need help with a communication challenge. Here's the situation: [Describe it in 2-4 sentences — what's happening, what's at stake, what you need people to do] My audience: [Who are you writing to? What do they care about? What's your relationship?] What I want to achieve: [What does success look like? A decision made? Trust restored?] What I'm worried about: [What could go wrong? What tone would be a mistake?] Don't write anything yet. First, ask me any clarifying questions you need. Then suggest an approach — the right format, the right framing, and what to lead with.

The last line is the key move. Asking the AI to think before it writes produces dramatically better output than going straight to a draft.

🔄 The workflow pattern you just learned

  • Give the full situation, not just the task. "Write an email to Marcus" is weak. "Write an email to Marcus, who approved this six months ago and needs to agree to a timeline extension" produces something genuinely useful.
  • Ask for a plan before a draft. "Don't write yet — suggest an approach first" forces a reasoning step before generation begins. It consistently produces better output.
  • Iterate in the same conversation. Don't start over when a draft is close. Say "good, but the second paragraph is too apologetic — make it more confident" and refine in place.
  • Ask for variants. "Give me a shorter version" or "give me a version for someone likely to push back" both work. You can get 2-3 strategic versions on request.
  • Let it flag what you're missing. Before sending anything high-stakes, ask: "What am I not thinking about? What's the strongest argument against this approach?"

🚀 What to build next

  • WorkflowWeekly update system — a standing prompt that takes your week's notes and produces a structured, audience-calibrated status update every Friday.
  • WorkflowMeeting prep briefer — paste in attendee names and an agenda; get talking points, likely questions, and what to watch out for.
  • GTMOutbound personalization system — a prompt that takes a prospect name, company, and a recent news item and produces a tailored outreach opening.
  • FinanceVariance narrative generator — paste in budget vs. actuals; get a plain-English explanation written for a non-finance executive audience.

Get your API Keys

Required

✦ Before you build anything — read this

At Hiya, which AI model you use depends on what data your tool will process. Claude (Anthropic) is for internal data only — things like meeting notes, internal documents, budget narratives, and drafts. Codex/OpenAI is required for anything that touches customer data or PII. Both run inside Cursor using the same workflow — you just select a different model. Many people end up using both: building and testing with Claude, then switching to an OpenAI model when the tool needs to process customer data. Get both keys now so you're not blocked later.

✦ A note on Cursor

This guide uses Cursor because it's the tool the guide's author uses and has been successfully adopted by others at Hiya. That said, if you already have a preferred IDE or are comfortable working in Terminal, both are valid alternatives — adapt these instructions for your own setup as needed.

🔑 Key 1: Anthropic API key — for internal data

This key connects Cursor to Claude. Use Claude-powered tools only for internal Hiya data — never for customer data or PII.

Go to the Anthropic Console

Visit console.anthropic.com and log in using your @hiya.com Google account. You should land inside Hiya's organization — you'll see the Hiya name in the top-left corner.

✦ Don't see Hiya's organization?

You haven't been added yet. Ask whoever manages Hiya's AI tools to add your @hiya.com email to the Anthropic org, then log in again.

Create a new API key

In the left sidebar, click "API Keys" then "Create Key." Name it something recognizable like your-name-cursor (e.g., alex-cursor). Click Create API Key.

Copy your key immediately

Your key will appear on screen as a long string of letters and numbers. Copy it now and save it somewhere private — a 1Password note or Apple Notes works well. This is the only time the full key is shown.

⚠ Keep it private

Never paste your key into Slack, email, or a shared doc. It's linked to Hiya's billing account. If you accidentally share it, delete it from the Console immediately and create a new one.

🔑 Key 2: OpenAI API key — for customer data

This key connects Cursor to OpenAI's models, including Codex. Under Hiya's Enterprise agreement, these models are approved for tools that process customer data or PII. Access requires an IT permissions request — start this now so you're not waiting when you need it.

Request API access from IT

Before you can generate an OpenAI API key, you need to be added to Hiya's OpenAI organization. Submit a request to IT asking to be added to the Hiya org on platform.openai.com. You'll receive an email invite once access is granted.

Accept the invite and sign in

Once you receive the invite, go to platform.openai.com and log in using your @hiya.com Google account. If Google login gives you trouble, try logging in with your API key directly instead.

Generate your API key

In the left sidebar, find API Keys and click "Create new secret key." Name it something recognizable like your-name-cursor. Copy it immediately and save it somewhere private — it won't be shown again.

⚠ Keep it private

Same rule as the Anthropic key — never share it in Slack, email, or a shared doc. It's tied to Hiya's OpenAI billing account.

Connect your Models in Cursor

Required

✦ One settings panel, two keys

You'll paste both API keys into Cursor's settings once. After that, switching between Claude and an OpenAI model is just a dropdown selection in the chat window — no re-configuration required.

Open Cursor Settings

Press Cmd + , or use the menu: Cursor → Settings → Cursor Settings.

Add your Anthropic key

Click "Models" in the left sidebar. Scroll to the Anthropic section, paste your Anthropic API key into the field, and press Enter. Cursor will confirm it's working.

Add your OpenAI key

In the same Models panel, scroll to the OpenAI section and paste your OpenAI API key. Press Enter to verify. Both providers will now appear as selectable models in your chat window.

Learn the model selector

Press Cmd + L to open the chat panel. Look for the model selector — usually a dropdown near the bottom of the chat input area. This is where you switch between Claude and OpenAI models. Select a Claude model (look for "Sonnet" or "Opus") to confirm your Anthropic key is working, then switch to a Codex or GPT model to confirm your OpenAI key is working.

🔀 When to use which model

  • Claude Use for building, testing, and any tool that processes internal Hiya data — documents, meeting notes, budget figures, internal communications. Claude is generally better at nuanced writing and reasoning tasks.
  • Codex / OpenAI Switch to a Codex or GPT model whenever your tool needs to process customer data or PII. Hiya's Enterprise agreement with OpenAI covers this data — Anthropic's does not. Use the model selector dropdown in the chat input to switch.
  • Both Many tools use both: build and iterate with Claude (no real data involved yet), then switch to an OpenAI model when you're ready to run the tool on actual customer records. Same tool, same Cursor window — just a different model selected.

Build a Document Summarizer ✦

Hands-on

✦ What you're building

A tool you can drop any document into — a contract, a meeting transcript, a long email chain — and get back a structured summary with key decisions, action items, and anything flagged for your attention. About 30 minutes. Once you've done it once, you know how to build almost anything else. This exercise uses internal data only, so you'll build it with Claude.

📁 Step 1: Create a project folder

Create a folder on your Desktop

Make a new folder — your Desktop works fine. Name it doc-summarizer or whatever you like.

Open it in Cursor

Go to File → Open Folder and select it. Cursor's left sidebar will show the empty folder. This is your workspace.

🤖 Step 2: Tell Claude what to build

Press Cmd + L to open the chat panel. Make sure you're in Agent mode — look for the mode selector and switch to Agent if needed. Select a Claude model from the model selector dropdown. Then paste this prompt:

Your first prompt to Claude
I want to build a simple document summarizer tool. Here's what it should do: 1. I paste in text (or point it at a file I've saved) 2. It produces a structured summary with: - A 2-3 sentence overview - Key decisions or conclusions - Action items and who owns them (if mentioned) - Any dates or deadlines - Anything I should flag for follow-up Please build this as a simple tool I can run from this folder. Make it easy to use — I'm not a developer, so walk me through each step as you go.

Don't worry about understanding what Claude creates. Your job is to describe what you want — Claude handles the rest.

▶️ Step 3: Run it and iterate

Let the AI build

Watch Agent mode work — it creates files in your folder, writes the code, and explains each step. Files appear in the left sidebar as it goes. Usually takes 1–2 minutes.

Follow the run instructions

Claude will tell you exactly how to run the tool. If any step is confusing, just ask: "Can you explain that more simply?"

Test it with internal content and refine

Paste in something from your actual work — internal documents only for now. If anything's off, tell Claude directly: "The action items section isn't picking up names — can you fix that?" Keep iterating until it works the way you want.

🚀 What to build next

The same pattern applies to everything: describe what you want, let the AI build it, test and refine. For anything that will process customer data, remember to switch the model selector to a Codex or GPT model before running it on real customer records.

  • FinanceBudget variance explainer — paste in a spreadsheet export and get a plain-English narrative explaining the variances, ready for a leadership update. (Internal data — use Claude.)
  • GTMCustomer email summarizer — summarize inbound customer emails and extract action items. (Customer data — switch to a Codex or GPT model before running on real emails.)
  • BothMeeting prep briefer — paste in a calendar event, attendee names, and context; get talking points and likely questions. (Internal data — use Claude.)
  • GTMCRM note formatter — turn rough call notes into structured CRM entries. (Customer data — switch to a Codex or GPT model before running on real customer records.)

The moves most people don't know

Working with AI agents

Reference

✦ These tips apply to every tool in this guide

The difference between someone who feels like AI is transforming their work and someone who still feels behind often comes down to a handful of behaviors — not tools. These are the moves worth internalizing.

🔄
When an agent tells you to do something — ask it to do it instead

If an AI agent says "Go to Settings and change X" or "Open Terminal and run Y," your first instinct should be to hand it back. Most of the time, the agent can do it for you — or at least walk you through it interactively.

Instead of following instructions manually, try: "Can you just do that for me?" — or — "How do I do that? Walk me through it step by step."
🤝
Use one AI to help you use another

If Cursor says it can't do something, or you get a confusing error, don't stop there. Copy the relevant part of the conversation — or take a screenshot — and paste it into ChatGPT or Claude.ai and ask: "How do I make Cursor do what I'm asking?" AI tools are surprisingly good at helping you navigate other AI tools.

Copy an error or stalled conversation from Cursor, then in ChatGPT: "Cursor is giving me this response. How do I get it to [what you want]?"
📸
Screenshots are context

You don't have to describe what you're looking at — just take a screenshot and paste it in. An error message, a confusing UI, a spreadsheet, a partially-built tool — pasting the image directly into the chat gives the AI far more context than a description would. This works in ChatGPT, Claude.ai, and Cursor.

Take a screenshot of anything confusing, paste it into the chat, and ask: "What am I looking at here, and what should I do next?"
📝
Write long prompts in Notes first

If you're crafting a detailed prompt or a long piece of context, write it in Apple Notes, Notion, or any text editor before pasting it into the AI chat. Browser windows close, tabs refresh, and sessions time out. Losing a carefully written prompt is frustrating — and avoidable.

Draft anything longer than a few sentences in Notes first. Paste it in when you're ready. Takes 10 seconds, saves real pain.
🎛️
You can teach agents how you want them to behave

As you use these tools more, you'll notice patterns in what you wish they'd do differently — "always ask before deleting anything," "explain what you're about to do before doing it," "never use bullet points." You can add these as standing rules to your system prompt or Project Instructions. Don't worry about getting this right now. It's something you discover over time, and the tools make it easy to add rules whenever something bothers you.

Prompt patterns that actually work

Reference

✦ The gap between generic and great output is almost always the prompt

Most people who feel like AI isn't delivering aren't using the wrong tool — they're giving it insufficient context. These patterns work across ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini regardless of which path you're on.

Summarize and extract
I'm going to paste in a [contract / email thread / document]. Please summarize it in 3-5 bullet points, then separately call out anything that needs a decision or action from me. [paste your content here]

Works for vendor agreements, board updates, long Slack threads exported to text.

Rewrite for a different audience
Rewrite the following for [a non-finance executive / a new sales rep / an external customer]. Keep it under [150 words]. Prioritize clarity over completeness. [paste your draft here]

Use this when you need to translate internal analysis into something shareable externally or cross-functionally.

Draft from scratch
Write a [type of document] for [audience / purpose]. Context: [one or two sentences about the situation] Tone: [professional and direct / warm and conversational / concise] Length: [a few sentences / one page / bullet points only]

The "tone" and "length" lines are the ones people skip — and they're what separate useful output from generic output.

Think it through with me
I'm trying to decide [describe the decision or problem]. Context: [relevant background] What I'm leaning toward: [your current thinking] What am I missing? What's the strongest argument against my current position?

Use this before a high-stakes meeting or when you're not confident you've thought something all the way through.

Already in your tools

Gemini in Google Workspace

No setup needed

✦ The AI that's already in the tools you use every day

Gemini is built into Gmail, Docs, Sheets, and Drive. Look for the ✦ Gemini button or sparkle icon in any Google app. No extra setup — and it's useful precisely because it doesn't require switching tabs.

  • Gmail "Help me write" drafts a reply based on the email thread. "Summarize this email" gives you a TLDR on a long chain. Both work directly inside the email view.
  • Google Docs Click the Gemini icon in the sidebar to ask questions about a document, rewrite sections, or generate new content in place — without leaving the doc.
  • Google Sheets Describe what you want in plain English — "Calculate the percentage change between column B and column C" — and Gemini suggests the formula. No spreadsheet expertise needed.
  • Google Drive Search across all your files with natural language — "Find the Q3 board deck" or "Show me docs about enterprise pricing" — without needing to recall exact file names.

✦ Use all three tools situationally

ChatGPT (Path A) for persistent workflow, Projects, and data analysis. Claude (Path B) for building tools and nuanced long-form writing. Gemini for in-context help inside Google without switching apps. Knowing when to reach for each one is the actual skill.
